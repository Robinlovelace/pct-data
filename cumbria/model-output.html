Data for cumbria were generated at 2016-05-15 by the National Propensity to Cycle Tool (PCT).

This is Version 1, developed by the PCT academic team and released with an GPL Affero Licence. This version examines cycling potential for main-mode commute trips using Census 2011 travel to work data and Middle-Level Super Output areas (MSOASs) as zones. There were 2.12993 × 105 commuters in the study area, all of which were included in area-based analysis. Where cycle flows are routed, flows within zones ( 19.27% of flows in cumbria ) are excluded as are commuters with no fixed workplace. Of the 407 between-zone regular commute flows, selection criteria were used. For OD pairs with maximum Euclidean distance of 20, flows with a minimum flow of 10 were selected. For OD pairs with maximum Euclidean distance of 7, flows with a minimum flow of 10 were selected. Adding those (overlapping) selection criteria resulted in 95.6% of all inter-zone commutes in the study area being selected, accounting for ( 55.94% of total commutes in cumbria). A modified version of the 2014 HEAT tool was used.

There are 407 OD pairs with origins and destinations in the study area. Of these, 357 meet the criteria that at least 10 people travelled to work along OD pairs up to 20 km in the 2011 Census. The additional selection criteria that at least 10 people travelled to work along OD pairs up to 7 km was met by 128 OD pairs. Adding those (overlapping) selection criteria resulted in 357 or 87.7% of all inter-zone OD pairs were selected in the region, accounting for 95.6% of inter-zone commutes in the study area.

Introduction

The results of the National Propensity to Cycle Tool (NPCT) scenarios are based on a model. This document presents information about the input data, model diagnostics, run time and the key outputs for each region. The aim is to inform users of the NPCT's interactive map how the results were generated. This document assumes some technical knowledge in the area of transport planning.

Both the pct and pct-shiny can be modified by others provided attribution to the original.
